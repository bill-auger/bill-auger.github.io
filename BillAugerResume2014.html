About the nerdy me:

    I got my first computer (a TI994A) as a young pup which may give you some indication of my age (read: wisdom).  When I was a schoolboy, I'm fairly certain that there was not a teacher within 50 miles who knew how to find the power switch on a computer (they were usually in the back or underneath - true story) so I taught myself to program TI-Basic; which is as I figure the best way to learn anything.  I can remember the day when I told my math teacher about a computer program that I wrote to do my homework for me; which caused him to become noticeably perplexed as to whether this was deserving of extra credit or detention.

    Around the age of 20 I took a 2000 hour certificate course in Electronics / Computer Repair (covering the gamut from electrons to machine code); which gave me the background in hardware that proved to be invaluable once I began learning the C language.

    Once the wonderful world web came around, I immediately took an interest in HTML and Javascript. Then about ten years ago I stumbled upon GNU/linux; so bash scripting was my next endeavor (it wasn't my fault - my Windows broke).  Although these were mostly hobbies for me until recently.

    About five years ago I decided to develop my long latent interest in programming more fully and began learning many other languages in other paradigms (Smalltalk , PHP , C# , Ruby , and LSL) and more recently thanks to edX and Coursera (Python , SML , Racket , ChucK , Oz, Prolog, Eiffel, and Haskell).  I've tried my best to put these in chronological order.  The Eiffel and Haskell courses have just begun at the time of this writing.

    Of the languages I've mentioned, I would say that by far the ones that to me were the most influential were Smalltalk and Racket.  When I first started learning about Smalltalk something 'clicked' with me and suddenly I 'got it'.  One of the books that I read at the time was "The Joy of Smalltalk"; which at first, struck me as a bit of an overstatement; but within a few months, I found that title to be quite accurate.  Finally, I had found a truly expressive language that seemed more musical than mathematical.  Instead of being purely logical, it seemed sensible; and rather than a rigorous tool you had to 'learn' by rote rudiment, it was a nice, fuzzy, flexible toy that you could 'understand' intimately through creative experimentation.  As I've heard it said: "Instead of writing code to make the machine happy, I could write code that made me happy.".  That, more than anything I think, fueled my new found passion in programming and my eventual gravitation toward Ruby , Rails , and the Agile Methodology.  Racket, I would like to give some 'props' as well for being a 'sensible' language in the functional paradigm; which was a welcome change after first learning SML.  It was in learning Racket that I became comfortable in the functional paradigm and finally able to appreciate Javascript; now that I am no longer crafting with it "against the grains", so to speak.
